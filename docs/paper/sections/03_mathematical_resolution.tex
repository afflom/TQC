\section{Mathematical Resolution and Computational Bounds}

Establishing topological invariance is a prerequisite; the subsequent requirement is demonstrating that the generated braid group representations are dense in the special unitary group, thereby achieving universal quantum computation capabilities.

\subsection{Modular Tensor Categories and the Quantum Double}
In TQC, the algebraic structure governing anyonic models is the Modular Tensor Category (MTC). An MTC is a braided monoidal category equipped with additional structure: it is ribbon (possessing compatible twists and duals), semi-simple, and possesses a non-degenerate $S$-matrix~\cite{bakalov2001}. The UOR Atlas leverages a specific MTC derived from the generalized quantum double $D(G)$, where $G$ is a finite group. Specifically, the framework constructs representations analogous to $D(\mathbb{Z}_O)$~\cite{drinfeld1987}, allowing the 24 classes to algebraically simulate the fusion and braiding rules of non-abelian excitations (such as Fibonacci anyons, where $\tau \otimes \tau = I \oplus \tau$).

\subsection{Density in the Special Unitary Group and Solovay-Kitaev}
The unitary representations of the braid group $B_n$ generated by the transformations among the 24 Atlas classes map into the special unitary group $SU(2^n)$. To claim universality, the generated set of unitaries must be capable of approximating any target unitary transformation. The conceptual model dictates the specific algebraic generators derived from the symmetry classes. 

Because the eigenspaces of these generators involve phases that are incommensurate with rational multiples of $\pi$~\cite{freedman2002}, the group they generate is mathematically dense in $SU(2)$. According to the Solovay-Kitaev theorem~\cite{dawson2005}, this density guarantees that any arbitrary quantum gate (a continuous unitary rotation) can be approximated to an arbitrary precision $\epsilon$ using a finite sequence of operations of length $O(\log^c(1/\epsilon))$, where $c \approx 3.97$. This mathematical resolution is explicitly validated by the repository's test matrix, which constructs sample approximation sequences to bounded precision against external F1 mathematical oracles.

\subsection{Delineation of Complexity Bounds: \texorpdfstring{$P$}{P} vs.\ \texorpdfstring{$BQP$}{BQP}}
It is crucial to accurately delineate the complexity-theoretic boundaries of this implementation. The classical complexity class $P$ contains decision problems solvable by a deterministic Turing machine in polynomial time. The class $BQP$ (Bounded-error Quantum Polynomial time) encompasses problems solvable by a quantum computer in polynomial time with a bounded error probability~\cite{bernstein1997}. 

The UOR Atlas runs deterministically on classical computational hardware (the holospace substrate). The exact classical simulation of generic quantum circuits is bounded by exponential overhead in time or space ($2^N$ amplitudes for an $N$-qubit state). Therefore, the UOR Atlas does not claim to execute arbitrary $BQP$ algorithms efficiently in polynomial classical time, as such a claim would implicitly assert $P = BQP$.

Instead, the framework acts as an exact, algebraically invariant validation ledger and topological compiler. It allows researchers to formally design, compile, and algebraically verify universal topological algorithms without the noise interference inherent to physical qubits. The execution of these algorithms scales classically, bounded by the tensor network contraction limits of the host machine, while preserving absolute logical fidelity.
